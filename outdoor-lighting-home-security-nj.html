The Deterrent Effect: What the Data Shows

60% of convicted burglars stated that the presence of outdoor lighting would cause them to choose a different target. — University of North Carolina at Charlotte, Department of Criminal Justice and Criminology

Lighting is the most consistently cited deterrent in surveys of convicted property offenders. More than alarm signs, cameras, or dogs, adequate lighting signals that a home is occupied, monitored, and risky to approach.

Why? Because light eliminates concealment. Burglars—especially the opportunistic residential type responsible for most break-ins—rely on being unobserved. They pick targets where they can approach, enter, and leave without being seen by neighbors, passersby, or cameras. Darkness is their ally.

A controlled study in New York City housing developments found that improved outdoor lighting reduced crime by 36% in treatment areas compared to control areas. — New York City Housing Authority / Vera Institute of Justice

The effect is consistent across study after study: well-lit exteriors are associated with significantly lower rates of property crime.

Where Traditional Security Lighting Falls Short

Most NJ homes have some form of outdoor lighting: a porch light by the front door, maybe a motion-sensor flood over the garage. These are better than nothing, but they have significant gaps:

1. Coverage Gaps

A single porch light illuminates the front entrance—roughly 15% of the home's perimeter. The remaining 85% (side yards, back of house, areas between windows, garage walls) stays dark. Burglars know this. Entry points shift to wherever the darkness is: back doors, basement windows, and side-yard first-floor windows are the most common forced-entry points in NJ burglary reports.

2. Motion-Sensor Fatigue

Motion-activated floods sound great in theory. In practice:

  • They trigger on animals, wind-blown debris, and car headlights, creating constant false activations that neighbors learn to ignore
  • They only activate after someone is already in range, not before they approach
  • Smart burglars know the reset time—move slowly through the zone, and many sensors won't re-trigger until a cooldown period passes
  • The sudden on/off pattern signals to everyone nearby that the light is motion-activated, not always-on—which reduces its psychological deterrent effect

3. Dark Zones Create Vulnerability

Any area of the home perimeter that isn't continuously illuminated is a potential approach route. Standard lighting setups almost always leave the sides and back of the home dark—exactly where most forced entries occur.

Dark Zones Around Your Home: A Quick Audit

Walk around your home after dark tonight with all your exterior lights on. Stand at the street and ask:

  1. Can you see every wall of the house clearly?
  2. Are there shadows along the sides where someone could walk unobserved?
  3. Is the back of the house visible from any neighbor's property or the street?
  4. Are first-floor windows illuminated enough that you'd notice someone near them?
  5. If your motion lights trigger, would a neighbor across the street actually notice?

If you answered "no" to even one of these, you have exploitable dark zones. Permanent perimeter lighting eliminates all of them simultaneously.

Crime Patterns in South Jersey

Residential burglary in South Jersey follows predictable patterns that outdoor lighting directly addresses:

  • Peak hours: 10 AM to 3 PM (when homes are empty) and 6 PM to midnight (when darkness provides cover). Permanent lighting addresses the evening window directly.
  • Peak seasons: Summer months and holiday travel periods—when more homes are unoccupied for extended periods. Vacation mode lighting is critical during these windows.
  • Entry methods: Forced entry through doors and first-floor windows accounts for 56% of residential burglaries nationally. These are the entry points most visible under perimeter lighting.
  • Target selection: Burglars report canvassing neighborhoods for easy targets—dark homes, unkempt yards, no visible security. Lighting is the most visible signal of a hardened target.

Beyond Burglary: Other Security Benefits

Permanent perimeter lighting also helps with:

  • Package theft: Porch pirates are less likely to approach a well-lit home in full view of neighbors and cameras
  • Vandalism: Illuminated exteriors see significantly less graffiti, property damage, and teen mischief
  • Slip and fall prevention: Even illumination around walkways and entries reduces the risk of nighttime trips and falls—a significant liability reduction for homeowners
  • Wildlife deterrence: Consistent lighting discourages raccoons, deer, and other animals from approaching the home's immediate perimeter
